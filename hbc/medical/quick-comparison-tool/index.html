Gary is generally healthy, and only visits his physician once aside from his annual physical/wellness exam. His cost of care for the year is $220, because the annual physical is at no-cost.

If Gary chooses the Gold or Silver Plan, Gary will spend nothing out of pocket, because he will be able to use the HBC contribution to his HSA to cover his medical costs. Gary will even have $280 remaining in his HSA for future health care costs. However, because of its low premiums, the Bronze Plan is the lowest-cost plan in this scenario, even though Gary will have some Out of Pocket costs.

Gary has type 2 diabetes, a condition that requires regular doctor visits, tests and insulin treatment. The cost of his treatment adds $4,000 to his medical expenses.

Gary will pay out of pocket if he chooses the Bronze, Silver or Gold Plan, but because of the lower deductible and Out of Pocket maximum in the Gold Plan, the Gold Plan is the lowest-cost plan in this scenario.

Gary breaks his arm in a bike accident. Physician fees, x-rays, a cast and other items cost $1,200.

The Bronze Plan Out of Pocket costs are the highest, but because of the lower premiums, the Bronze Plan is the lowest-cost Plan in this scenario.

For an additional $192 in annual premium, Gary is protected from major accidental injuries.

Gary has a serious heart attack, and the cost is very high – about $9,000 for this example.

Gary will pay out of pocket if he chooses the Bronze, Silver or Gold Plan, but because of the lower deductible and Out of Pocket maximum in the Gold Plan, the Gold Plan is the lowest-cost plan in this scenario.

Fortunately, Gary has $5,000 in critical illness coverage that he purchased for $4 per month (premium rates depend on age and tobacco status). The critical illness benefit is paid to him directly when he is diagnosed. It covers all of his costs and some is left over for incidental expenses.

They use medical services only when absolutely necessary and see their primary care physician once a year for routine sicknesses. Mike has high cholesterol and takes a generic statin.  Medical expenses for the year total $340.

Under the Gold and Silver Plans, they pay nothing out of pocket, as their medical costs are covered by HBC’s contribution into their HSA. Even better, they save $660 that will stay in their HSA for future health needs. Because of its lower premium, the Bronze Plan is the lowest-cost plan in this scenario.

Mike has a heart attack, and the cost is very high – about $9,000 for this example. Along with the other routine family medical costs, their total cost of health care is lowest with the Silver Plan.

Each family member ends up at their primary care doctor’s office once per year for common illnesses. One of the Johnson children sees an allergy specialist once per year and Mr. Johnson takes generic cholesterol medication. Medical expenses for the year total $780.

Under the Gold and Silver Plans, they pay nothing out of pocket, as their medical costs are covered by HBC’s contribution into their HSA. Even better, they save $220 that will stay in their HSA for future health needs. Because of its low premium, the Bronze Plan is the lowest cost plan in this scenario.

Mr. Johnson has type 2 diabetes, a condition that requires regular doctor visits, tests and insulin treatment. Their total cost of medical care for the year is an additional $4,000. They pay more than twice as much out of pocket with the Bronze Plan, but its premium makes it the lowest cost plan overall.

A fall off a bike results in a broken arm for one of the Johnson children. Physician fees, x-rays, a cast and other items cost $1,200 in the end.

The Bronze Plan Out of Pocket costs are the highest but the total cost of this plan is still the lowest because of the premium. The Gold Plan has the lowest Out of Pocket costs due to the lower deductible and the HSA contribution.

If the Johnsons had accident insurance, his Out of Pocket costs are $0 under the Gold and Silver plans and reduced greatly under the Bronze Plan.

Mr. Johnson has a heart attack, and the cost is very high – about $9,000 for this example. Along with the other routine family medical costs, their total cost of health care is lowest with the Silver Plan and slightly higher with the Gold and Bronze plans.

Fortunately, the Johnsons purchased $5,000 in critical illness coverage for about $8 per month (premium rates depend on age and tobacco status). The critical illness benefit is paid to Mr. Johnson directly when he is diagnosed. It covers all of his costs and some is left over for incidental expenses.

Note: all costs are annualized estimates. Your cost will likely be different. Out of Pocket Expenses include deductibles and coinsurance. HSA assumes receipt of both the automatic and wellness incentive contributions. All scenarios assume the Standard care expenses are still part of the equation. Costs assume use of in-network providers. Out-of-network services are more costly and significantly impact the total cost of care.
